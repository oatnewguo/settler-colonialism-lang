From our investigations into our collocation analysis, and from our close readings, we argue the following. In comparison to our corpus of private letters, the published books we examined

  1. contain proportionally more characterizations of indigenous peoples disappearing,
  2. present less evidence of their current and continuing presence in the region, and
  3. de-legitimize to a greater extent their indigeneity and their right to land.

We can begin to trace this in our verb collocates. Let's take another look at them.

Notice that in these lists of verbs most significantly associated with indigenous people, 8 out of 30 are in the past or past participle tense in the published books, compared with only 4 out of 30 in the private letters. Many of these are verbs referring to the disappearance of indigenous people, such as "extinguished," "purchased," "fled," and "resided." In numerous cases, these verbs painted an over-simplistic picture of settler colonization, one in which indigenous lands were ceded, sold, conquered, or simply abandoned as the indigenous populations inhabiting them left or disappeared. In contrast, in the published books, just 2 out of 30 verbs are gerunds—words ending with "-ing," implying a continued action or state of being—while in the private letters, 4 out of 30 are gerunds. That's twice as many, and they express not only the continuing inhabitancy of indigenous people—as with "residing" and "inhabiting," which are in fact significant words in the corpus of published books as well—but also the continuing difficulty and urgency of the existing situation in which settler colonizers lived next to indigenous communities, one that required constant diplomacy and understanding of messy settler-indigenous relations. Narrative transfer (II)

Narrative transfer (II): when a "tide of history" rationale is invoked to deny legitimacy to ongoing indigenous presences and grievances. This transfer focuses on "fatal impacts", on indigenous discontinuity with the past, and typically expresses regret for the inevitable "vanishing" of indigenous people. If they have had their last stand, if their defeat is irretrievably located in the past, their activism in the present is perceived as illegitimate.

Veracini, Settler Colonialism: A Theoretical Overview, 2010

By the strategy of narrative transfer (II), settler colonizers distinguish a past that included an indigenous presence from a postcolonial present in which indigenous people have disappeared. While the current presence of indigenous communities was certainly well-known to most white Americans between 1771 and 1837 and could not be wholly erased in this way, narrative transfer (II) worked in nuanced ways in our published books to deny legitimacy to indigenous presences and grievances. In particular, we discovered that our published books depicted the vanishing of indigenous people as an inevitable, ongoing, and natural process. In doing so, they also often claimed that indigenous people had simply vanished, whether by disease, violence, or migration, from specific areas of land they had once inhabited. The portrayals of the western frontier that these books presented to readers were ones in which great quantities of land were newly empty and unclaimed for white Americans to settle, and more lands would be available in the future as indigenous people vanished.

Vanishing by treaty

The Indian title, by various treaties, has been extinguished to about 70,000 square miles . . .

Brown, The Western Gazetteer; or, Emigrant's Directory, 1817

. . . the estimated amount of unsold lands, on which the foreign and Indian titles had been extinguished, within the limits of the new States and Territories, was 227,293,884 acres . . .

Peck, A New Guide for Emigrants to the West, 1837

Often, indigenous people were described as vanishing as they ceded or sold their land in treaties with the United States. While it is true that indigenous lands were signed away to the United States in treaties, this was by no means as simple a process as one would have gleaned from our published books.
